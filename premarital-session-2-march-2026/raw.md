# Communication, Conflict, and Forgiveness

## Purpose

To normalize conflict and equip the couple to handle it with humility, clarity, and grace.

## Key Scriptures

James 1:19–20; Proverbs 15:1; Ephesians 4:25–32; Matthew 18:21–35

James 1:19–20 (LEB): 19 Understand this, my dear brothers: every person must
be quick to hear, slow to speak, slow to anger, 20 for human anger does not
accomplish the righteousness of God.

Proverbs 15:1 (LEB): A soft answer will turn away wrath, but a word of trouble will stir anger.

Ephesians 4:25–32 (LEB): 25 Therefore, putting aside the lie, speak truth each one of you with his neighbor, because we are members of one another. 26 Be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun set on your anger, 27 nor give place to the devil. 28 The one who steals must steal no longer, but instead must labor, working with his own hands what is good, so that he may have something to share with the one who has need. 29 No rotten word must proceed from your mouth, but only something good for the building up of the need, in order that it may give grace to those who hear, 30 and do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, by whom you were sealed for the day of redemption. 31 All bitterness, and rage, and wrath, and clamor, and abusive speech, must be removed from you, together with all wickedness. 32 Become kind toward one another, compassionate, forgiving one another, just as also God in Christ has forgiven you.

Matthew 18:21–35 (LEB): 21 Then Peter came up to him and said, “Lord, how many times will my brother sin against me and I will forgive him? Up to seven times?” 22 Jesus said to him, “I do not say to you up to seven times, but up to seventy times seven! 23 “For this reason the kingdom of heaven may be compared to a man—a king—who wanted to settle accounts with his slaves. 24 And when he began to settle them, someone was brought to him who owed ten thousand talents. 25 And because he did not have enough to repay it, the master ordered him to be sold, and his wife and his children and everything that he had, and to be repaid. 26 Then the slave threw himself to the ground and began to do obeisance to him, saying, ‘Be patient with me, and I will pay back everything to you!’ 27 So the master of that slave, because he had compassion, released him and forgave him the loan. 28 But that slave went out and found one of his fellow slaves who owed him a hundred denarii, and taking hold of him, he began to choke him, saying, ‘Pay back everything that you owe!’ 29 Then his fellow slave threw himself to the ground and began to implore him, saying, ‘Be patient with me and I will repay you!’ 30 But he did not want to, but rather he went and threw him into prison until he would repay what was owed. 31 So when his fellow slaves saw what had happened, they were extremely distressed, and went and reported to their master everything that had happened. 32 Then his master summoned him and said to him, ‘Wicked slave! I forgave you all that debt because you implored me! 33 Should you not also have shown mercy to your fellow slave as I also showed mercy to you?’ 34 And because he was angry, his master handed him over to the merciless jailers until he would repay everything that was owed. 35 So also my heavenly Father will do to you, unless each of you forgives his brother from your hearts!”

## Themes

Conflict reveals the heart; it does not create sin.

Patterns of hostility matter more than isolated arguments.

- But do not allow yourself to over-generalize in your frustration: "He always ...", "She never ..."
- I probably struggled with this in reading far too much into arguments and taking Kassia's frustration with me way too personally, creating hostility when there was only frustration

True repentance and forgiveness are essential marital practices.

- Your stuck with your person, are you going to stay frustrated forever?
- Remind yourself of the gospel at every argument, "Jesus has forgiven me so much more than I am/feel owed by my spouse right now"

## Discussion Prompts

What is your instinct under tension: withdrawal, pursuit, defensiveness?

What makes it hard for you to apologize or forgive?

- For me it is correctness: I find it hard to apologize for trying to be correct (or understood) when that pursuit ends up hurting Kassia in some way

## Practical Emphasis

Listening before responding.

Speaking clearly without accusation.

- Classic advice to avoid "you" statements, use "I" statements
- I might recommend "reviewing" fights after the fact, as a team see what went well or poorly and what could use work for the next time

Addressing issues at appropriate times.

- Sometimes it has to wait a day
