  ## Possible Titles

  ## Working Angle


  This should probably be a personal essay, not a manifesto about AI. The core tension:


  - agents made me dramatically more productive

  - that productivity increased the responsibility I carry

  - the financial upside I used to realize personally has gone down, not up


  ## Section Stub: Opening Scene


  Prompt:


  - Start with a concrete moment. Maybe reviewing a massive PR from the intern / contractor
  side of the system.

  - What did I feel in that moment: confusion, dread, pressure, responsibility, skepticism?

  - What was the specific thought: "I might own all of this later, even if I did not
  really produce or validate all of it myself."

  - Can I open with one sharp sentence that names the tension right away?


  Possible opener idea:


  "Agentic coding has made me faster than ever, but it has also made me feel responsible
  for more than I can honestly hold in my head."


  ## Section Stub: This Is About Me, Not Everyone


  Prompt:


  - Add a strong caveat early: this is not prediction, prescription, or universal
  truth.

  - Name my context: big company, internal platform work, political environment, mixed
  team ownership, frontier models via Copilot.

  - What about my temperament matters here? Hyperfocus, initiative, problem solving,
  pressure sensitivity, desire to meet expectations.

  - What would I want a reader to not mishear?


  ## Section Stub: Who I Was Before Agents


  Prompt:


  - Briefly describe my work history and self-understanding.

  - I am not formally trained, so what made me valuable anyway?

  - What does it mean that people were impressed before AI too?

  - Pull in relevant threads from `pages/blog/forge-ahead.md`: prior projects, practical
  problem solving, being useful, learning systems by doing.

  - What gifts do I think God gave me that made me effective before this shift?


  Questions to answer:


  - What kind of worker was I before agents entered my workflow?

  - What did I already know how to do well?

  - What part of my value came from focus rather than brilliance?


  ## Section Stub: Why I Leaned Into Agentic Coding


  Prompt:


  - Explain that I did not stumble into this accidentally; I chose to go hard.

  - Why did it feel natural to me to adopt agents aggressively?

  - What about the mission at Caterpillar Autonomy made the leap feel worth it?

  - Pull in `pages/blog/cat-autonomy-2-0.md`: I stayed because the work felt meaningful,
  not merely because it was interesting.

  - Mention harness engineering / Nexus / experimentation if it helps show this was
  a deliberate investment.


  Questions to answer:


  - Why did I trust this direction enough to reorganize my work around it?

  - Was I chasing novelty, usefulness, recognition, mission, or all of the above?


  ## Section Stub: What Actually Changed


  Prompt:


  - Describe the practical change in my day-to-day work.

  - I am no longer mainly writing code line by line; I am directing, reviewing, testing,
  and integrating generated work.

  - What changed about the quantity of output?

  - What changed about how coworkers and managers perceived me?

  - What changed about the kind of questions people started asking me?


  Questions to answer:


  - When did I realize this was not just autocomplete, but a new mode of working?

  - What does "enterprise-grade work fully written and produced by agents" actually
  mean in practice?


  ## Section Stub: The Hidden Cost of Speed


  Prompt:


  - Write about the gap between output and understanding.

  - I can ship more, but I do not always know the code as intimately as hand-written
  systems I built before.

  - Testing is not the same as ownership.

  - Documentation existing is not the same as confidence.

  - What kinds of future problems does speed create?


  Questions to answer:


  - What do I know I have not fully read?

  - What kinds of bugs or maintenance traps am I afraid I am creating?

  - What does it feel like to build something that works but still feels partially
  foreign?


  ## Section Stub: Responsibility Without Clear Boundaries


  Prompt:


  - This may be the emotional center before the money section.

  - Describe the contractor / intern dynamic and why it intensifies the burden.

  - Their output can become my long-term maintenance problem.

  - The organization sees me as someone who understands AI and agents, so responsibility
  pools onto me.

  - Ask whether the pressure is explicit or self-imposed, but note that the body feels
  it either way.


  Questions to answer:


  - Who is actually accountable when AI-assisted code breaks six months from now?

  - What does it mean to be treated like the local expert when I do not feel like
  one?

  - Why is being noticed beginning to feel costly instead of rewarding?


  ## Section Stub: The Financial Irony


  Prompt:


  - State this clearly and concretely.

  - For years, I realized more value from my output because I worked two jobs concurrently.

  - Due to the new role, new responsibility, and the demands of agentic coding in
  this environment, I had to go down to one job.

  - So the irony is: the tools make me dramatically more productive, but I personally
  realize less economic upside from my own gifts and skills than I did before.

  - Keep this grounded and honest, not whiny.


  Questions to answer:


  - What exactly have I lost financially?

  - Why does this feel bigger than "I want more money"?

  - Where do the gains of AI go when an individual contributor gets faster?

  - If I can now produce 10x more code, why does my life not feel lighter, freer,
  or better compensated?


  Possible thesis sentence:


  "Agentic coding increased the value I create at work, while decreasing the share
  of that value I personally get to realize."


  ## Section Stub: Mission, Meaning, and Fatigue


  Prompt:


  - Reaffirm the good: this is meaningful work, and that matters to me.

  - Tie back to autonomy, safety, and the reasons I stayed at Caterpillar.

  - Then tell the truth: meaning does not cancel fatigue.

  - What has already started to feel tired, dulled, or overdrawn in me?


  Questions to answer:


  - What purpose did this role awaken in me?

  - Why does that sense of purpose already feel threatened?

  - How do I talk honestly about fatigue without pretending I am fully burned out
  if I am not sure?


  ## Section Stub: Questions I Cannot Yet Answer


  Prompt:


  - Let the essay breathe here instead of forcing a neat conclusion.

  - Gather the real unresolved questions.

  - If I stopped using agents, would I still impress people?

  - Would I solve fewer problems or create fewer future ones?

  - Am I carrying real expectations or anticipated ones?

  - Is this making me a better engineer, or just a faster source of output and responsibility?


  ## Section Stub: Conclusion


  Prompt:


  - End modestly, not with a manifesto.

  - I am not anti-AI.

  - I am not saying agents are bad.

  - I am trying to tell the truth about what this transition has cost me personally.

  - Land on the mismatch between speed, responsibility, and reward.


  Possible closing direction:


  - The tools are real.

  - The gains are real.

  - The responsibility is real.

  - The redistribution of value is also real.

  - I am still trying to understand what faithfulness looks like inside that tension.


  ## Material To Weave In


  - My lack of formal software engineering background

  - My history of being useful by solving practical problems quickly

  - My tendency toward hyperfocus and over-responsibility

  - The shift from hand-written confidence to agent-mediated uncertainty

  - The contractor / intern maintenance concern

  - The drop from two incomes to one

  - The irony that Cat benefits more from my increased output than I do

  - The Christian framing around calling, providence, stewardship, and fatigue


  ## Things To Avoid


  - sounding like I am making universal claims about all AI use

  - sounding bitter without being specific

  - centering line-count metrics too much without translating them into responsibility

  - pretending I have fully landed on a conclusion

  - making this a complaint post about coworkers instead of a reflection on burden
  and change'
